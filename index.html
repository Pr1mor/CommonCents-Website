§ 01 — Vision

The
problem.

Most budgeting happens after the damage is done — a receipt shoved in a wallet, a spreadsheet touched once a month, a regret at the checkout.

CommonCents flips the loop. Every transaction is logged in under 30 seconds, and the dashboard answers one question instantly: am I on track? It's a tool for the moment before the purchase, not the month after.

🎓
University students

Managing loans and limited allowances with zero formal financial training.

💼
Freelancers

Irregular income streams that demand strict, real-time expense discipline.

§ 02 — What it does

Major
features.

Every feature is backed by a dedicated service in the business layer and a real Android SQLite implementation with a stub twin for testing.

01
Rapid transaction entry

Log a purchase in under 30 seconds. Amount, category, date, description — done.

02
Analytics dashboard

Pie charts, bar charts, cumulative trends, and daily net savings — all offline.

03
Spending limits & alerts

Set per-category monthly caps and get home-page alerts before you overshoot.

04
Budget projection

Project end-of-month savings from current spending velocity vs. your income.

05
Recurring automations

Set rent, subscriptions, and paydays to log themselves on a schedule.

06
Search & sort

Filter transactions by category, date range, amount, or free-text description.

07
Import / Export

Move data in and out via CSV. Your records never leave your device unless you say so.

08
Multi-user accounts

Hashed credentials, per-user data isolation, private budgets on a shared device.

09
100% offline & private

No cloud, no telemetry, no accounts to create. Your money data stays on your phone.

§ 05 — Development postmortem

What we
learned.

Architecture

Three clean layers.

CommonCents follows a strict three-layer architecture. The business layer never touches Android APIs; the persistence layer ships with both a stub in-memory implementation and a real SQLite implementation behind the same interfaces, which made every service unit-testable without an emulator.

▲ presentation/
~20 Activities · home, analytics, add/edit, profile, search, limits, imports
│
◆ business/services/
11 managers · Financial, Analytics, Automation, BudgetProjection, Export, Import, SearchSort, SpendingLimit, User, Validation…
│
▼ persistence/
7 interfaces · stubdatabase/ for tests + realdatabase/ SQLite for production
Iteration 2 retrospective

The improvement area.

What changed

We shifted from reactive bug-fixing to proactive architectural planning. We stopped treating maintainability as a post-release chore and began refactoring during development—specifically breaking down our monolithic Data interface and centralizing our validation logic.

Was it successful?

Highly successful. By addressing the root architectural issues (like Interface Segregation Principle violations) rather than just patching surface-level bugs, we removed immense friction from our development pipeline and made the codebase truly modular.

Evidence

Because of the decoupled architecture, new features were integrated without modifying unrelated interfaces. Additionally, our estimation accuracy improved drastically: Iteration 1 had a 27% margin of error, but by Iteration 3, our predictable velocity shrank that error margin to just 12%.

✓ What went well
  • Stub + real DB split. Every service was unit-tested against the stub long before SQLite existed — zero business logic ever blocked on the database.
  • Interface-first persistence. Swapping the real repository in was a single DI change, not a rewrite.
  • Test culture. 62+ test files across unit and instrumented suites gave us confidence to refactor late in the term.
  • Iteration 3 UI Unification. Because our Presentation and Business layers were strictly decoupled, we were able to completely overhaul the app's visual design system in the final weeks without breaking a single backend test or service.
✗ What we'd change
  • Design Smell: We noticed a leaky architectural boundary late in development where our Business layer became coupled to native Android UI classes.
  • Technical Debt: We left the Multi-Currency Transaction feature as outstanding technical debt, deferring the necessary API integrations and database schema changes to a future release.
  • Effort Imbalance: In early iterations, we spent a disproportionate amount of time patching Presentation layer bugs rather than building reusable Business layer logic.
  • Starting Over: If we restarted from Iteration 0, we would enforce a strictly pure-Java Business layer from day one to prevent Android framework dependencies from breaking our standard unit tests.
Lessons learned

Teamwork & large projects.

The most valuable lesson we learned was that large projects survive on process, not just individual coding ability. Early in the term, code merged in isolation often broke the `main` branch; implementing strict pull request reviews and enforcing that code must pass the stub test suite before merging completely eliminated these late-stage regressions. We also learned that regular, honest scrums are essential for negotiating scope and managing technical debt, ensuring we delivered a highly polished core product rather than a fragmented, overly ambitious one.
